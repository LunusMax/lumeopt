\begin{abstract}
Football is characterized by high levels of uncertainty, which makes squad planning and player recruitment complex decision-making processes. 
This study investigates the use of Machine Learning (ML) and Operations Research (OR) techniques as decision-support tools for football squad composition. 
A predictive model based on a multilayer perceptron (MLP) is trained to estimate the final number of points obtained by football teams in a season using 
individual player statistics from the previous season. 
The predicted team performance is then used as the objective function in heuristic optimization algorithms that simulate player recruitment under budget constraints. 
Experiments conducted using data from major European leagues demonstrate the potential of combining predictive modeling and 
optimization to support squad planning decisions. The proposed framework provides a structured and data-driven approach to evaluating recruitment strategies, 
reducing subjectivity in player acquisition and contributing to more informed decision-making in professional football.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Football is the most popular sport in the world \cite{Almulla2023}. Over the past decades, the sport has undergone significant technological and analytical 
development, accompanied by substantial economic growth in the global football industry \cite{Rodrigues2022}. Despite these advances, football remains highly 
unpredictable. A notable example is Leicester City's unexpected triumph in the Premier League (PL) in 2016, an outcome that surprised analysts 
and fans worldwide \cite{Baboota2019}.

Given the economic relevance and global popularity of football, both academic researchers and industry professionals have increasingly explored 
data-driven approaches to support decision-making in the sport \cite{Baboota2019}. The financial magnitude of football is reflected in the transfer market, 
which moves billions of dollars annually. For instance, between June and September 2023, global player transfers reached approximately USD 7.3 billion according 
to FIFA reports \cite{Globo2023}. These large financial investments highlight the importance of improving decision-making processes related to player recruitment 
and squad construction.

In the field of Computer Science, several studies have investigated the use of statistical analysis and machine learning (ML) techniques to model 
patterns and generate predictions in complex environments. Joseph, Fenton and Neil (2006), for example, investigated the use of Bayesian networks and 
other ML techniques to predict match outcomes for Tottenham Hotspur during the 1995–1997 seasons. In professional football practice, data-driven strategies 
have also been adopted by clubs such as Brighton \& Hove Albion, which has implemented a recruitment model strongly supported by statistical analysis. This 
approach has enabled the identification of undervalued players in the transfer market, generating substantial financial returns through successful 
transfers \cite{BBC2023Caicedo, CNN2023Caicedo, ESPN2023}.

In academic research, most studies applying ML to football focus primarily on predicting match outcomes, estimating win probabilities, or optimizing 
betting strategies. However, comparatively fewer studies address the problem of squad composition and player recruitment using data-driven 
approaches \cite{Abidin2021}. To the best of our knowledge, few studies attempt to predict season-level team performance from individual player statistics 
and incorporate these predictions into a formal squad optimization framework. This gap is particularly relevant because squad construction requires 
evaluating multiple technical, physical and contextual factors simultaneously, including team playing style, league characteristics and player career 
stage \cite{Abidin2021}. Furthermore, the stochastic nature of football performance means that even seemingly optimal transfers may fail to 
deliver the expected results.

Transfer market inefficiencies further illustrate the complexity of these decisions. According to Graham (2024), a transfer can be considered 
successful if the player starts at least 50\% of matches during the first two seasons with the club, a concept referred to as the ``50\% rule''. Based on 
this criterion, approximately 46\% of transfers above EUR 10 million in the PL between 1992 and 2021 can be classified as unsuccessful \cite{Graham2024}. These 
outcomes are often associated with factors such as tactical incompatibility, inaccurate evaluation of player ability, positional mismatches, coaching 
decisions or physical issues.

Recent advances in ML have significantly expanded the capacity to analyze sports data, enabling the identification of patterns that were previously 
difficult to detect in large datasets. However, predictive models do not eliminate the inherent uncertainty of football; rather, they serve as decision-support 
tools that can help reduce risk and support strategic choices.

To address this challenge, this study proposes an integrated framework that combines machine learning-based performance prediction with heuristic squad optimization.

The main contributions of this work are threefold. First, we develop a machine learning model capable of predicting season-level team performance 
from individual player statistics. Second, we integrate these predictions into an optimization framework that simulates squad recruitment decisions under 
budget constraints. Third, we demonstrate through computational experiments that the proposed approach can identify recruitment strategies capable of improving 
predicted team performance.

\section{Related Work}

The application of ML in sports analytics has grown substantially over the past decades, particularly in football. 
According to \cite{Horvat2020}, ML techniques are well suited for sports data analysis due to the large volume of structured and unstructured information 
generated from match events, tracking systems and historical databases. Consequently, numerous studies have investigated predictive models for football performance.

Most of the existing literature focuses on predicting the outcome of individual matches, typically framed as a classification problem involving win, 
draw or loss. Early work by \cite{Joseph2006} employed Bayesian networks to model match outcomes using a dataset of Tottenham Hotspur matches between 1995 and 1997. 
More recent studies have explored a broader range of machine learning algorithms and larger datasets. For example, \cite{Baboota2019} compared multiple 
supervised learning methods, including Random Forest, Support Vector Machines and Gradient Boosting, to predict PL match outcomes. 
Similarly, \cite{Awadallah2020} evaluated deep learning models such as dense neural networks and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks using data from ten PL seasons. 
These studies demonstrate the potential of ML techniques for modeling football outcomes, but they remain primarily focused on predicting individual matches.

Beyond match-level prediction, some research has investigated the determinants of team performance over an entire season. 
Statistical analyses have shown that aggregated performance indicators such as goals scored, shots on target and possession are associated with 
final league standings \cite{LagoBallesteros2010}. Economic studies have also examined the relationship between financial investment and team performance, 
indicating that variables such as wage expenditure can influence competitive outcomes \cite{Carmichael2011}. While these studies contribute to 
understanding factors associated with success in football leagues, they generally rely on retrospective analyses and do not explicitly model team 
performance as a function of the individual composition of the squad.

In parallel with predictive modeling, operations research (OR) techniques have been applied to various decision-making problems in sports. 
These applications include scheduling competitions, optimizing tournament structures and analyzing tactical decisions \cite{Kendall2010}. 
Many of these problems involve combinatorial optimization and high computational complexity, often requiring heuristic or metaheuristic approaches 
to obtain feasible solutions.

More directly related to team composition, several studies have investigated optimization-based approaches to player selection. \cite{Tavana2013} 
proposed a fuzzy inference system for selecting players and determining tactical formations, while \cite{Qader2017} formulated player selection as a 
multicriteria decision-making problem based on performance indicators. Other works have modeled squad selection using mathematical programming approaches, 
including stochastic programming models for team composition \cite{Pantuso2017} and assignment-based formulations for player-position allocation \cite{Boon2003}. 
Although these studies advance the formal modeling of squad selection problems, their objective functions are typically based on 
predefined performance scores or expert-defined evaluations.

Recent developments in prescriptive analytics emphasize the integration of predictive models with optimization methods to support decision-making processes 
\cite{Bertsimas2019}. In this paradigm, machine learning models are not only used to generate predictions but also incorporated directly 
into optimization frameworks that guide strategic decisions.

Despite these advances, the integration between predictive models of team performance and optimization-based squad composition remains 
relatively unexplored in the football analytics literature. To the best of our knowledge, few studies attempt to predict season-level team 
performance directly from individual player statistics and incorporate these predictions into a formal squad optimization framework.
This study addresses this gap by proposing a framework that combines machine learning-based prediction of team performance with an 
optimization procedure designed to simulate player recruitment decisions under budget constraints.

\section{Data and Feature Engineering}

The dataset used in this study consists of player performance statistics collected from two major European football leagues: the English PL
and the Italian Serie A. These competitions were selected due to their high level of competitiveness and the availability of detailed player performance data.

The data were obtained from the FBref football statistics platform through web scraping procedures implemented in Python. The dataset covers five seasons, 
from 2019--2020 to 2023--2024, including all teams that participated in the first division of each league during this period. 
The dataset contains 200 team-season observations and 4,000 player-season records.

Each observation corresponds to the statistical performance of a player during a specific season while representing a particular team. The dataset contains 
47 player performance attributes describing both offensive and defensive contributions, including advanced football statistics such as goals, 
assists, shots, passes and defensive actions.

To construct the predictive framework, player statistics from season \(t-1\) were associated with the final number of points obtained by each team in
season \(t\). This temporal alignment ensures that all explanatory variables precede the target variable, preventing the use of future information during
model training.

The two leagues were used separately during the modeling process. Specifically, the ML model was trained using data from one league and evaluated
using data from the other. This cross-league strategy allows the assessment of model generalization across distinct competitive environments while avoiding
information leakage between training and test samples.

All data processing and experiments were implemented in Python using standard ML libraries.

\section{Performance Prediction Model}

To predict team performance, a ML model was developed using individual player statistics as explanatory variables. Since the original
dataset contains 47 performance attributes per player, dimensionality reduction was applied not only to mitigate multicollinearity among player 
statistics but also to reduce the dimensionality of the learning problem and improve model generalization.

The number of principal components was determined using a scree plot analysis. As shown in Figure 1, the cumulative explained variance reaches approximately
97.66\% with 20 components, after which additional components provide only marginal gains. Therefore, 20 principal components were selected.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/pca_variance.png}
\caption{Cumulative explained variance obtained through principal component analysis (PCA). The first 20 components explain approximately 97.66\% of the total variance.}
\label{fig:pca_variance}
\end{figure}

After dimensionality reduction, each player is represented by a vector of 20 principal components. To construct a team-level representation, each
squad was truncated to the 20 players with the highest playing time during the season. Consequently, each team is represented by a vector containing
the PCA components of its 20 players, resulting in 400 player-related features.

Additionally, the number of points obtained by the team in the previous season was included as an explanatory variable, producing a final input
vector with 401 attributes.

The overall prediction framework used in this study is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ml_pipeline}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\input{figs/pipeline}
\caption{Overview of the proposed framework integrating machine learning-based performance prediction and squad optimization.}
\label{fig:ml_pipeline}
\end{figure}

Because the ordering of players within a squad does not carry intrinsic meaning, a data augmentation strategy was employed to generate multiple
equivalent representations of each team. This procedure consisted of randomly permuting the ordering of players within the input vector,
generating multiple training samples representing the same squad while preserving the original statistical properties of the dataset.

Several machine learning algorithms were evaluated for the task of predicting team performance from player statistics. In addition to the
Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), experiments were conducted using Linear Regression (LR), Random Forest (RF), and Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost).
These models represent different families of predictive approaches, including linear models, ensemble methods based on decision trees,
and neural networks.

The comparative evaluation indicated that the MLP achieved the best overall predictive performance in terms of the coefficient of determination
($R^2$) and prediction error metrics. For this reason, the MLP was selected as the predictive component of the proposed framework and was used
as the objective function in the subsequent squad optimization stage.

The selected MLP architecture consists of two hidden layers with 128 and 32 neurons, respectively, both using ReLU activation functions.
Batch normalization and dropout layers were incorporated to improve generalization and mitigate overfitting. The output layer contains a
single neuron with linear activation, producing the predicted number of points obtained by the team at the end of the season.

The models were trained using data from one league and evaluated using data from the other league, ensuring that training and testing were
performed on distinct competitions. This strategy prevents information leakage between datasets and provides a more realistic evaluation of
the model's generalization capability.

In regression problems, a natural baseline model consists of predicting the mean value of the target variable for all observations. In the context of this

Although the predictive model explains approximately 55\% of the variance in team performance, the prediction task remains inherently challenging.
Football outcomes are influenced by multiple contextual factors that are not directly captured by individual player statistics, such as tactical strategies,
injuries, managerial decisions, and interactions between players during matches.

Nevertheless, the obtained results demonstrate that aggregated player performance indicators contain meaningful information about team-level outcomes,
making the predictive model suitable for use as an objective function in the squad optimization framework proposed in this study.

\section{Squad Optimization}

Once the ML model capable of predicting the final number of points obtained by a team in a season is identified, the second stage of the framework
focuses on optimizing squad composition. In this phase, the problem is formulated as a constrained optimization task in which different recruitment strategies are
evaluated according to the predicted team performance generated by the machine learning model.

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:optimization_pipeline}, the optimization stage begins with the current squad and a pool of candidate players. 
The algorithm simulates potential recruitment decisions and evaluates each resulting squad configuration using the machine learning model under 
budget constraints.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\input{figs/optimization_pipeline}
\caption{Pipeline of the squad optimization procedure. Recruitment simulations are evaluated using the machine learning model under budget constraints.}
\label{fig:optimization_pipeline}
\end{figure}

Let $P$ denote the set of candidate players available in the transfer market and let $S$ represent the current squad of a team. 
Each player $i \in P$ has an associated synthetic market value $c_i$ and performance attributes derived from the previous season. 
The objective is to select a subset of players $X \subseteq P$ whose recruitment maximizes the predicted team performance while 
respecting the available transfer budget $B$.

Formally, the optimization problem can be expressed as:

\begin{equation}
\max_{X \subseteq P} f(S \cup X)
\end{equation}

subject to:

\begin{equation}
\sum_{i \in X} c_i \leq B
\end{equation}

where $f(\cdot)$ represents the machine learning model responsible for predicting the final number of points obtained by the team in the target season.

\subsection{Synthetic Player Market Value}

To incorporate financial constraints into the optimization process, a synthetic market value was defined for each player based on age and aggregated statistical
performance. The value assigned to player $i$ is computed as:

\begin{equation}
V_i = 1000 \cdot \left( 1000 \cdot \max(0, 30 - I_i) + 10 \cdot \sum_{j=1}^{n} x_{ij} \right)
\end{equation}

where $I_i$ represents the age of player $i$ and $x_{ij}$ corresponds to the $j$-th statistical attribute associated with the player.

The age component assigns higher values to younger players, reflecting the typical valuation dynamics of football transfer markets, in which younger
players tend to retain higher resale potential and longer career horizons. The second term aggregates player performance through the sum of available
statistical indicators.

The external scaling factor is applied solely to produce values with magnitudes compatible with transfer budget constraints used during the optimization
procedure. This synthetic valuation does not aim to reproduce real market prices, but rather to provide a consistent cost function suitable for the optimization
framework proposed in this study.

\subsection{Greedy Recruitment Algorithms}

Two greedy heuristics were implemented to iteratively construct a set of player recruitments.

Starting from the current squad, the algorithm evaluates candidate players from the previous season while excluding athletes already present in the team or
previously selected during the optimization process. Each candidate is evaluated using the machine learning model that predicts the final number of points
obtained by the team.

To maintain a constant squad size during evaluation, newly recruited players replace the same number of players with the lowest playing time in the original
squad. A candidate is considered feasible only if its synthetic market value respects the available transfer budget.

Two variants of the greedy algorithm were tested. In the \textit{first-improvement} strategy, candidates are evaluated sequentially and the first player that improves
the objective function is accepted. In the \textit{best-improvement} strategy, all feasible candidates are evaluated and the player producing the largest
improvement in predicted team performance is selected.

\subsection{Local Search Optimization}

In addition to greedy heuristics, local search algorithms were implemented to explore the solution space more thoroughly.

The procedure starts from a randomly generated feasible solution composed of players from the previous season whose combined market value respects the
available budget. To ensure computational tractability, a pool of 100 candidate players is randomly sampled from the full dataset.
This sampling strategy was adopted to maintain computational tractability while preserving diversity among candidate players.

The neighborhood of a solution is defined through swap operations, in which a player currently selected for recruitment is replaced by another candidate not
yet chosen. A swap is considered feasible if the resulting squad respects the budget constraint.

Two acceptance strategies were evaluated. In the \textit{first-improvement} variant, the search stops as soon as a better neighboring solution is found.
In the \textit{best-improvement} variant, all neighbors are evaluated and the swap producing the largest improvement in the objective function is selected.

The iterative search process terminates when no feasible swap produces further improvement in the predicted team performance, indicating convergence to a local
optimum in the explored solution space.

This section presents the experimental results obtained from the squad optimization procedures described in the previous section. The objective

Since the optimized squads represent hypothetical recruitment scenarios, no real-world outcomes are available for comparison. Therefore, solution
quality is measured exclusively through the objective function defined by the predictive model.

\section{Discussion}

The results obtained in this study demonstrate the potential of combining machine learning and optimization techniques to support decision-making
in football squad construction. The predictive model was able to explain approximately 55\% of the variability in the final league points of teams
based solely on individual player performance statistics from the previous season. Considering the inherent uncertainty of football
competitions and the absence of tactical and contextual variables in the model, this level of explanatory power suggests that aggregated player
statistics contain meaningful information about collective team performance.

The optimization experiments illustrate how these predictions can be incorporated into decision-support tools for player recruitment.
The results show that heuristic algorithms are capable of identifying player combinations that improve the predicted performance of teams
while respecting financial constraints. In scenarios with larger budgets, greedy algorithms were able to identify high-quality
solutions, whereas under tighter financial constraints local search methods produced more competitive results.

These findings highlight the importance of considering both predictive accuracy and search strategy when integrating machine learning models
into optimization frameworks. The predictive model defines the quality landscape explored by the algorithms, while the optimization procedure
determines how efficiently promising solutions are discovered within the search space.

Overall, the proposed framework demonstrates how performance prediction models can be combined with heuristic optimization to simulate player
recruitment scenarios and support data-driven decision-making in professional football.

However, the proposed framework still presents some limitations. The predictive model relies exclusively on individual player statistics and
does not explicitly capture tactical interactions between players or contextual factors such as injuries and managerial decisions. Future work may explore
richer representations of team dynamics to improve predictive accuracy.

\section{Conclusion}

This study proposed a framework that integrates machine learning and optimization techniques to support football squad planning. A predictive
model based on a multilayer perceptron was developed to estimate team performance using individual player statistics from the previous season.
The model achieved an explanatory power of approximately $R^2 = 0.55$, indicating its ability to capture relevant patterns linking player
performance and collective team outcomes.

The predicted team performance was then used as the objective function in a squad optimization problem under budget constraints. Experimental
results showed that heuristic algorithms can identify recruitment strategies capable of improving the predicted performance of teams while
respecting financial limitations.

These results suggest that integrating predictive modeling and optimization can provide a structured and data-driven approach to squad
construction in professional football. By transforming player performance data into quantitative decision-support tools, the proposed
framework contributes to reducing subjectivity in recruitment processes and advancing the application of analytical methods in sports
management.

Future work may explore the incorporation of additional contextual variables, such as tactical information and player interactions, as well
as the application of more advanced optimization techniques to further improve squad selection strategies.
