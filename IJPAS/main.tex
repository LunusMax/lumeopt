\begin{abstract}

Football is characterized by high levels of uncertainty, which makes squad planning and player recruitment complex decision-making processes. 
This study investigates the use of Machine Learning (ML) and Operations Research (OR) techniques as decision-support tools for football squad composition. 
A predictive model based on a multilayer perceptron (MLP) is trained to estimate the final number of points obtained by football teams in a season using 
individual player statistics from the previous season. 
The predicted team performance is then used as the objective function in a local search optimization framework that simulates player recruitment 
under budget constraints. Experiments conducted using data from major European leagues demonstrate the potential of combining predictive modeling and 
optimization to support squad planning decisions. The proposed framework provides a structured and data-driven approach to evaluating recruitment strategies, 
reducing subjectivity in player acquisition and contributing to more informed decision-making in professional football.

\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} sports analytics, football, machine learning, squad optimization, decision support

\section{Introduction}
% ------------------------------------------------

Football is the most popular sport in the world \cite{Almulla2023}. Over the past decades, the sport has undergone significant technological and 
analytical development, accompanied by substantial economic growth in the global football industry \cite{Rodrigues2022}. Despite this evolution, 
football remains highly unpredictable. A notable example is Leicester City's unexpected triumph in the English Premier League (EPL) in 2016, an outcome
 that surprised analysts and fans worldwide \cite{Baboota2019}.

Given the economic relevance and global popularity of football, both academic researchers and industry professionals have increasingly explored 
data-driven approaches to support decision-making in the sport \cite{Baboota2019}. The financial magnitude of football is reflected in the transfer market, 
which moves billions of dollars annually. For instance, between June and September 2023, global player transfers reached approximately R\$36.6 billion according 
to FIFA reports \cite{Globo2023}. These large financial investments highlight the importance of improving decision-making processes related to player 
recruitment and squad construction.

In the field of Computer Science, several studies have investigated the use of statistical analysis and Machine Learning (ML) techniques to model 
patterns and generate predictions in complex environments. Joseph, Fenton and Neil (2006), for example, explored the use of Bayesian networks and other 
ML techniques to predict match outcomes for Tottenham Hotspur between 1995 and 1997. In professional football practice, data-driven strategies have also been 
adopted by clubs such as Brighton \& Hove Albion, which has implemented a recruitment model strongly supported by statistical analysis. 
This approach has enabled the identification of undervalued players in the transfer market, generating substantial financial returns through successful transfers 
\cite{BBC2023Caicedo, CNN2023Caicedo, ESPN2023}.

In academic research, most studies applying ML to football focus primarily on predicting match outcomes, estimating win probabilities, or optimizing 
betting strategies. However, comparatively fewer studies address the problem of squad composition and player recruitment using data-driven approaches \cite{Abidin2021}. 
This gap is particularly relevant because selecting players for a squad requires evaluating multiple technical, physical and contextual factors simultaneously, 
including team playing style, league characteristics and player career stage \cite{Abidin2021}. Furthermore, the inherent uncertainty of football means 
that even seemingly optimal transfers may fail to deliver the expected performance.

Transfer market inefficiencies illustrate the complexity of these decisions. According to Graham (2024), a transfer can be considered successful 
if the player starts at least 50\% of matches during the first two seasons with the club, a concept referred to as the ``50\% rule''. Based on this criterion, 
approximately 46\% of transfers above €10 million in the EPL between 1992 and 2021 can be classified as unsuccessful \cite{Graham2024}. 
These outcomes are often associated with factors such as tactical incompatibility, inaccurate evaluation of player ability, positional mismatches, 
coaching decisions or physical issues.

Recent advances in ML have significantly expanded the capacity to analyze sports data, enabling the identification of patterns that were previously 
difficult to detect in large datasets. However, predictive models do not eliminate the inherent uncertainty of football; rather, they serve as decision-support 
tools that can help reduce risk and support strategic choices.

Within this context, this study investigates the use of predictive models to estimate the final number of points obtained by football teams in a season based 
on historical individual player performance statistics. The machine learning model converts player-level performance indicators from the previous season into 
predictions of team-level performance in the subsequent season. Unlike approaches that focus solely on match outcome prediction or isolated player evaluation, 
the proposed framework analyzes players as components of a collective system whose combined performance influences the final league standing. 
The predictions generated by the model are used as the objective function in an optimization procedure that simulates player recruitment decisions 
under budget constraints.

The main contribution of this work is the integration of a machine learning model that predicts team points with an optimization framework 
designed to simulate squad recruitment decisions under budget constraints.

\section{Related Work}
% ------------------------------------------------

The application of machine learning (ML) in sports analytics has grown substantially over the past decades, particularly in football. 
According to \cite{Horvat2020}, ML techniques are well suited for sports data analysis due to the large volume of structured and unstructured information 
generated from match events, tracking systems and historical databases. Consequently, numerous studies have investigated predictive models for football performance.

Most of the existing literature focuses on predicting the outcome of individual matches, typically framed as a classification problem involving win, 
draw or loss. Early work by \cite{Joseph2006} employed Bayesian networks to model match outcomes using a dataset of Tottenham Hotspur matches between 1995 and 1997. 
More recent studies have explored a broader range of machine learning algorithms and larger datasets. For example, \cite{Baboota2019} compared multiple 
supervised learning methods, including Random Forest, Support Vector Machines and Gradient Boosting, to predict English Premier League (EPL) match outcomes. 
Similarly, \cite{Awadallah2020} evaluated deep learning models such as dense neural networks and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks using data from ten EPL seasons. 
These studies demonstrate the potential of ML techniques for modeling football outcomes, but they remain primarily focused on predicting individual matches.

Beyond match-level prediction, some research has investigated the determinants of team performance over an entire season. 
Statistical analyses have shown that aggregated performance indicators such as goals scored, shots on target and possession are associated with 
final league standings \cite{LagoBallesteros2010}. Economic studies have also examined the relationship between financial investment and team performance, 
indicating that variables such as wage expenditure can influence competitive outcomes \cite{Carmichael2011}. While these studies contribute to 
understanding factors associated with success in football leagues, they generally rely on retrospective analyses and do not explicitly model team 
performance as a function of the individual composition of the squad.

In parallel with predictive modeling, operations research (OR) techniques have been applied to various decision-making problems in sports. 
These applications include scheduling competitions, optimizing tournament structures and analyzing tactical decisions \cite{Kendall2010}. 
Many of these problems involve combinatorial optimization and high computational complexity, often requiring heuristic or metaheuristic approaches 
to obtain feasible solutions.

More directly related to team composition, several studies have investigated optimization-based approaches to player selection. \cite{Tavana2013} 
proposed a fuzzy inference system for selecting players and determining tactical formations, while \cite{Qader2017} formulated player selection as a 
multicriteria decision-making problem based on performance indicators. Other works have modeled squad selection using mathematical programming approaches, 
including stochastic programming models for team composition \cite{Pantuso2017} and assignment-based formulations for player-position allocation \cite{Boon2003}. 
Although these studies advance the formal modeling of squad selection problems, their objective functions are typically based on 
predefined performance scores or expert-defined evaluations.

Recent developments in prescriptive analytics emphasize the integration of predictive models with optimization methods to support decision-making processes 
\cite{Bertsimas2019}. In this paradigm, machine learning models are not only used to generate predictions but also incorporated directly 
into optimization frameworks that guide strategic decisions.

Despite these advances, the integration between predictive models of team performance and optimization-based squad composition remains 
relatively unexplored in the football analytics literature. In particular, few studies attempt to estimate season-level team performance as a function of 
individual player statistics and subsequently incorporate these predictions into formal optimization models.

This study addresses this gap by proposing a framework that combines machine learning-based prediction of team performance with an 
optimization procedure designed to simulate player recruitment decisions under budget constraints.

\section{Data}
% ------------------------------------------------

The dataset used in this study consists of player performance statistics collected from two major European football leagues: the English Premier League
and the Italian Serie A. These competitions were selected due to their high level of competitiveness and the availability of detailed player performance data.

The data were obtained from the FBref football statistics platform through web scraping procedures implemented in Python. The dataset covers five seasons, 
from 2019--2020 to 2023--2024, including all teams that participated in the first division of each league during this period.

Each observation corresponds to the statistical performance of a player during a specific season while representing a particular team. The dataset contains 
47 player performance attributes describing both offensive and defensive contributions, including advanced football statistics such as goals, 
assists, shots, passes and defensive actions.

To construct the predictive framework, player statistics from season \(t-1\) were associated with the final number of points obtained by each team in
season \(t\). This temporal alignment ensures that all explanatory variables precede the target variable, preventing the use of future information during
model training.

The two leagues were used separately during the modeling process. Specifically, the machine learning model was trained using data from one league and evaluated
using data from the other. This cross-league strategy allows the assessment of model generalization across distinct competitive environments while avoiding
information leakage between training and test samples.

\section{Performance Prediction Model}
% ------------------------------------------------

To predict team performance, a machine learning model was developed using individual player statistics as explanatory variables. Since the original
dataset contains 47 performance attributes per player, dimensionality reduction was first applied using Principal Component Analysis (PCA).
The number of principal components was determined using a scree plot analysis, resulting in the selection of 20 components, which explain
97.66\% of the total variance of the dataset.

After dimensionality reduction, each player is represented by a vector of 20 principal components. To construct a team-level representation,
each squad was truncated to the 20 players with the highest playing time during the season. Consequently, each team is represented by a vector
containing the PCA components of its 20 players, resulting in 400 player-related features. Additionally, the number of points obtained by the team
in the previous season was included as an explanatory variable, producing a final input vector with 401 attributes.

Because the ordering of players within a squad does not carry intrinsic meaning, a data augmentation strategy was employed to generate multiple
equivalent representations of each team. This procedure consisted of randomly permuting the ordering of players within the input vector,
generating multiple training samples representing the same squad while preserving the original statistical properties of the dataset.

The predictive model was implemented using a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP). The network consists of two hidden layers with 128 and 32 neurons,
respectively, using ReLU activation functions. Batch normalization and dropout layers were applied to improve generalization and reduce the risk
of overfitting. The output layer contains a single neuron with linear activation, producing the predicted number of points obtained by the team
at the end of the season.

The models were trained using data from one league and evaluated using data from the other league, ensuring that training and testing occur on
distinct competitions. This strategy prevents information leakage between datasets and provides a more realistic evaluation of the model's
generalization capability.

\section{Squad Optimization}

The predictions generated by the model are used as the objective function in an optimization procedure that simulates 
player recruitment decisions under budget constraints.

A local search algorithm was implemented to explore the solution space through
